# The Pale Vault — Complete Dungeon Design

---

## Theme: The Cost of Greed

Greed has a price. It's never paid by the one who profits.

The baron mined the salt that made the region wealthy. The miners who dug the deepest veins paid with their lives. The order declared the salt sacred and taxed the region for protection. The people they "protected" paid with their freedom and prosperity. The lich claimed the dead as a workforce to solve a crisis the previous eras' greed created. The dead paid with their rest.

Each era's greed was rational. The baron needed the income — he'd taken on debts to expand. The order needed the authority — the institution required revenue. The lich needed the workforce — the barrier was genuinely failing. None were wrong about what they needed. They were wrong about what it cost, because the cost was never theirs to pay.

Now the player arrives. The mine rewards taking: better weapons, better armor, more options, more power. The player who loots thoroughly is stronger. That's the trap — the mine has ALWAYS rewarded taking. That's why no one could stop.

The cost is never mechanical. The game doesn't punish looting with stat penalties. The cost is visible: names on bone tags, an ancestor's unsent letter, a cultist who has nowhere else to go, a grave robber who dies alone in the dark. The player sees who pays, and the price accumulates as they descend. By the time they face the lich — rich, powerful, well-equipped — they must decide whether to continue the pattern or break it.

The question the game asks: **who pays for what you take?**

---

## Game Structure

**The dungeon is the entire game.** There is no town, no shop, no safe hub. The player enters the mine and does not leave until an ending is reached or they die.

**Flow:** Title screen -> Intro (4 slides establishing the heir's motivation) -> Area 1, Room 1 -> explore, fight, discover -> Ending slides -> Title.

**Why no town:** A town lets the player extract value and retreat to safety - literally enacting the greed cycle. Removing it means everything the player finds stays in the dangerous space. The question "what do you do with what you found?" IS the theme.

**Starting equipment:** A knife. Nothing else. The heir enters the mine with what they could scrape together. Everything else - weapons, consumables, abilities - is found in the dungeon.

**Death:** Die -> "You have fallen" screen -> reload from last checkpoint (state saved on room entry, before combat). The player retries from the previous room. They can retreat, rest, choose different paths. The consequence is time and learning, not currency.

**Safe room rest:** Rest heals 5% max HP per turn in unsafe rooms, with ambush risk (AI ticks). In safe rooms (sunlit, warded, or brazier-lit), rest heals 15-20% with NO ambush risk. This makes salt spending on wards directly feed survival.

## Salt: The Core Resource

**Gold does not exist as a mechanic.** Coins and treasure may appear as narrative props, but they have no mechanical value. The only resource is **salt** - the mine's product, the barrier's material, and the reason every era went deeper.

**Finding salt:** Salt deposits in walls, order stores, refined caches, regrowing crystals (Area 4). The mine is a salt mine - salt is everywhere, in varying quantities and qualities.

**Spending salt (3 mechanics):**
- **Ward a room** (primary): Pour salt along a threshold or trace the order's ward pattern. Permanent. Enemies won't enter or spawn in warded rooms. This IS the order's core technology - the player does what the order did, with their own hands. Warded rooms also become safe rest points.
- **Fuel a brazier** (secondary): Spend salt to light or strengthen a sacred brazier. Creates a true-light source and a checkpoint. Light is the core contested resource.
- **Narrative interactions** (specific): A few deep-area props accept salt - reinforce barrier sections, make offerings, repair old mechanisms. These affect ending flags.

**Hoarding salt:** The number goes up. It feels like progress. Games teach you to collect resources. Then at the ending, the game asks how much you took. The salt hoard influences ending availability - high salt = greed endings, low salt (spent on wards/survival) = selfless endings. The design already requires "low loot/aggression ratio" for Ending 4. Salt IS the loot ratio.

**The tension IS the theme:** The baron extracted salt for profit instead of safety. The order extracted salt for tithes instead of wards. Now the player finds salt and makes the same choice: survival tool or treasure to keep? Every salt spent on a ward is salt you can't take home. Every salt hoarded is a room left dangerous.

**Salt never gates progression.** All progression gates remain capability-based (blunt weapon, fire source, Crystal Lantern, etc). Salt spending is purely for optional safety and ending weight. A player who spends zero salt can still reach every required area and ending.

## Abilities

Combat abilities are found in the dungeon, not purchased at buildings. The order had all these capabilities - their facilities are in the mine.

| Ability | Found | Location context |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **heal** | Sacred brazier room / order chapel | Area 2 |
| **holy_smite** | Great Brazier or consecrated shrine | Area 2 |
| **aimed_shot** | Scout post or training room | Area 1-2 |
| **counter_stance** | Knight training hall | Area 2 |
| **stealth** | Shadow-infested areas / scout manuals | Area 3 |
| **acid_flask** | Alchemist's lab | Area 3-4 |
| **smoke_bomb** | Lab or cultist supply cache | Area 3-4 |

Each ability is learned through a prop interaction that makes narrative sense - the character LEARNS from the place, not "unlocks" from a menu.

## What Lies Below

At the base of the salt deposit, deeper than any gallery has reached, the boundary between the material world and something else has worn thin. Not a creature. Not a mind. A pressure — vast, patient, geologically ancient. The salt deposit formed around this weak point over millennia, the way calcium builds around an irritant. The salt IS the containment. Natural, geological, older than civilization.

The presence draws. The salt closest to the weak point is the purest, the most crystalline, the most valuable. It glows faintly. It resonates when struck. It is warm. The presence refines the salt around it, concentrating it, perfecting it. **The thing that makes the deep salt worth mining is the thing the salt is containing.**

This is the mine's original sin: the treasure and the danger are the same substance. Every ton of deep salt extracted thins the barrier. The more you take, the closer the presence comes to the surface. The most profitable mine in the region is the most dangerous place in the world — and the danger is proportional to the profit.

The Demon in the sealed chamber (Area 4) is a fragment of the presence given form — the part of it closest to being a creature. Not the presence itself, which is vast and impersonal, but a splinter. Breaking the seal lets the splinter loose. The full presence breaking through would be catastrophic on a regional scale.

**The presence threads through every era:**
- **Era 1 (Baron):** Miners report heat, sounds, bad dreams in the deep galleries. The baron's journal: *"Superstition does not appear on the ledger."* The catastrophic collapse happens when a deep gallery breaks through a critical salt stratum. The presence surges. Hundreds die. The rubble re-seals the breach.
- **Era 2 (Order):** The order investigates the rubble-sealed breach. They find the presence. They study it. They build wards to reinforce the natural salt barrier — real, necessary work for the first generation. Then they realize the threat is leverage: *"Pay your tithes, or the darkness rises."* They resume mining the upper salt for income. Each generation mines deeper. The barrier thins. The wards compensate, mostly.
- **Era 3 (Lich):** Serevic inherits a barrier thinned by 250 years of extraction, maintained by wards 150 years past their designed lifespan, in an institution that no longer has the skill to repair either. The presence is stronger than it's been since the collapse.
- **The player discovers (Area 4):** In unmined galleries, the salt is growing back. Regrowing. Healing. The natural containment is sufficient IF the mining stops. The mine heals itself if humans leave it alone. This knowledge makes the final ending possible — and is the one fact the lich cannot accept.

## The Hymn: The Dungeon's Emotional Throughline

The Order of the Pale Vigil fused singing with light. Sacred braziers were lit during choral performances — the melody and the flame as one ritual. The chapel's natural salt-crystal acoustics made the hymns transcendent. Pilgrims crossed the region to hear them.

The hymn was the one part of the order that wasn't transactional. Everything else — the tithes, the mining licenses, the recruitment, the salt monopoly — served the institution's greed. The hymn served nothing. It was just beautiful.

The lich killed the singing. Not from malice — from efficiency. The dead can't sing. Singing doesn't maintain the barrier. The brazier rituals were aesthetic, not structural. Serevic cut everything that didn't serve the maintenance mission.

Now:
- **Banshees wail corrupted fragments of the Vigil Hymn.** The melody is recognizable underneath the scream. The most beautiful thing the order created, reduced to a weapon.
- **Sacred braziers** can only be relit by performing the melody. Fire and song together, as they were designed.
- **Prior Rennic** (ghost scholar, Area 2) remembers the hymn. When the player hums a fragment learned from a hymnal, Rennic harmonizes — and for a moment, warmth in a dead place.
- The **Great Brazier** in the chapel cavern, when relit with the full hymn, restores sound and light simultaneously. The stalactites carved as singing saints catch the resonance. The chapel sounds alive.
- During the lich encounter, performing the hymn is the only thing that breaks Serevic's composure. Not because it reminds them of family. Because it reminds them of what they cut. The one thing in the mine with no price, no function, no place in a maintenance schedule — and they destroyed it because their calculus had no column for beauty.

## Story

### Era 1: The Baron (~400 years ago) — The Greed of Ambition

Baron Aldric Ashvere found the salt deposit. The first decade was genuine prosperity: fair wages, safe galleries, a growing town. Schools were built. The baron was admired.

Then the surface veins thinned. Deeper salt was richer — purer, warmer, almost luminous — but harder and more dangerous to extract. Meanwhile, other regional salt sources undercut prices. The baron had expanded the town on projected income; he owed creditors in the capital. He couldn't slow down without defaulting.

The debt made the next extraction necessary. The next extraction funded the next expansion. The expansion created the next debt. Not villainy — arithmetic. The baron didn't decide to kill his workers. He decided to service his debts, and the deaths were a line item he couldn't afford to see.

**What the player discovers:**
- Indentured labor contracts for the deepest galleries (foreman's office, Area 1). Workers who owed the baron for housing, tools, food — debts structured so they could never be repaid.
- Safety petitions from the miners' guild, stamped "REVIEWED" and filed without action (baron's study, Area 1).
- A collapsed ventilation shaft in the deep galleries. The repair cost would have delayed a shipment. The baron delayed the repair.
- Miners reporting heat, vibrations, and dreams in the lowest levels. The baron's journal: *"Superstition does not appear on the ledger."*

The collapse killed the baron and 347 miners. The mine swallowed them. The surviving family inherited the debts without the income. Bankrupt within a generation. The mine rights sold to the Order of the Pale Vigil for twelve silver marks — the price of a horse.

**The greed:** Taking more than the mine could safely give because the debts demanded it.
**Who paid:** The miners. 347 dead. The baron died too, but incidentally — he happened to be in the wrong gallery. The miners had no choice about where they stood.

### Era 2: The Order (~250 years ago) — The Greed of Authority

The Order of the Pale Vigil arrived two generations after the collapse. They excavated the upper galleries, found the rubble-sealed breach, and studied the presence below. Their early work was genuine: they built wards to reinforce the natural salt barrier, established a monastery in the mine's upper levels, trained knights to maintain the seal.

Then they discovered what the threat could do for them.

The order told the region: *"Something terrible sleeps beneath the salt. Only we can keep it sealed. Pay your tithes, send your children to serve, obey our authority — or the darkness rises."* Half true. The presence was real. The wards helped. But the natural salt containment did most of the work. The order took credit for geology.

**Specific mechanisms of exploitation the player discovers:**
- **Salt tithes.** Every town in the region paid annual tribute for "blessed salt" and protection. The blessed salt was regular salt with a prayer said over it. The protection was the wards — which the towns never saw and couldn't verify.
- **Mining licenses.** The order controlled who could mine the upper galleries at steep fees. The deep galleries were off-limits — not for safety, but because the deep salt's unusual purity was the order's most profitable product.
- **Knowledge hoarding.** The restricted archive contained the true nature of the deep threat, the geological surveys, the ward maintenance schedules. Classified. The public got vague warnings about "darkness." Knowledge was leverage.
- **Recruitment pressure.** "Send your children, or the darkness rises." The best recruits went to the wards (useful). Most went to administrative and enforcement roles (parasitic).

**What hollowed the order:** The gap between the story and the reality. "Sacred guardians" vs. "armed tax collectors who happened to maintain a ward." Genuine believers saw the gap and left or died. Cynics and careerists stayed. Each generation, the ratio shifted. The wards degraded — not dramatically, but steadily, because the people maintaining them cared less. Administrative staff grew. The warrior class shrank. Tithes increased. Services decreased.

The order also resumed mining the upper salt for income beyond tithes. Each generation mined a bit deeper, told themselves the wards would compensate, and filed the geological surveys without reading them.

Their defining practice — the choral hymn — drew pilgrims from across the region. The beauty was real. The pilgrims who came for the hymns also paid offerings. The offerings funded the institution. Even the beauty served the machine.

**The greed:** Authority justified by a threat they inflated, maintained by ignorance they enforced.
**Who paid:** The region. Generations taxed for protection that was largely geological. Young people conscripted into an institution that had forgotten its purpose. And the barrier — the one thing the order existed to protect — slowly, silently degrading because the people responsible were busy counting tithes.

### Era 3: The Lich (~150 years ago) — The Greed of Competence

Serevic joined the order young — an orphan raised in the monastery. Brilliant. Meticulous. The first person in three generations to actually READ the restricted archive.

What they found: geological surveys, ward maintenance logs, degradation reports that nobody had filed in decades. Serevic did what no one had bothered to do — they measured the barrier. They ran the numbers.

The barrier had thinned by 40% since the order's founding. The wards — designed to supplement the salt, not replace it — were compensating for salt that had been mined out. The wards themselves were degrading. At current rates: barrier failure within 30–50 years. A surge from the deep, worse than the baron's collapse, because the containment was weaker and the presence had been pressing for centuries.

Serevic presented their findings to the order's council. The response: *"It's been fine for two hundred years. It'll be fine for two hundred more. Raise the tithe if you're worried."*

Serevic tried to reform the order. End the salt extraction. Divert resources to ward repair. Train new ward-keepers. The council refused. Ending extraction would eliminate their income. Training ward-keepers would mean admitting the existing wards were failing. Admitting the wards were failing would undermine the authority that justified the tithes. **The institution couldn't fix the problem because fixing it would require admitting it existed, and admitting it existed would destroy the institution.**

Serevic watched the numbers get worse for five years. Then they found the transformation texts in the restricted archive and did the math one last time.

- The barrier needs repair. Repair requires constant, skilled, tireless labor.
- Living workers need food, pay, rest, motivation. The order can't provide these.
- Dead workers need none of it. Dead workers work forever.
- The order has centuries of dead buried in its crypt. Ready-made workforce.
- The transformation makes Serevic immortal — able to oversee the repair indefinitely.
- Cost: Serevic's humanity. The remaining living order members. The dead's rest.

**Serevic became a lich because they did the math, and the math said "become a monster."**

They raised the order's dead. Extinguished the sacred flames — the dead don't need light. Silenced the singing — the dead can't sing. Expanded the crypt into an industrial ossuary. Replaced firelight with coldfire — functional illumination, no fuel cost, no fire risk. Began systematic barrier repair.

And it worked. For 150 years, the undead workforce has rebuilt and maintained the salt barrier. The presence has been contained. The numbers stabilized. By any material measure, Serevic made the correct decision.

**The greed:** "Only I can do this" → "Only I should do this" → "Only I will do this" → "Only I exist to do this." The greed of the indispensable: the refusal to believe anyone else can handle the work. Serevic claimed everything — the dead, immortality, absolute authority — because they were the only competent person in a failing institution.
**Who paid:** The dead, who never consented to being raised. The living members of the order, who were killed or driven out. The surface towns, who lost their mine and their economy. The cultists, living people who serve the lich because the lich's greed destroyed their alternatives.

**The irony (Area 4):** In unmined galleries, the salt is growing back. The natural containment heals — slowly, geologically, but measurably. If the mining had simply STOPPED after the collapse, the salt would have repaired itself over centuries. The order's continued extraction made the problem worse. Serevic's solution is necessary only because greed made it necessary. The correct answer was always to stop taking.

But Serevic can't accept this. They've spent 150 years optimizing a system. They ARE the system. If the mine heals on its own, then their sacrifice was pointless. The dead were enslaved for nothing. The order was destroyed for nothing. Serevic would rather maintain the vigil forever than confront the possibility that it was unnecessary.

## The Lich: Grandmaster Serevic

Serevic has been alone for 150 years. The last living person they spoke to was a cultist, decades ago. The player arriving is the most significant event in a century.

**Personality:**
- **Precise** — Thinks in systems, schedules, rotations. Everything quantified. "The northwest ward requires recalibration every 73 days. I have not missed one."
- **Impatient** — 150 years of managing a workforce that can't answer back. Zero tolerance for imprecision. "The council said 'it's fine.' I showed them the numbers. They chose not to read them."
- **Bitter** — Saved the region. No one knows. No one thanks them. "I solved the problem your ancestors created. You're welcome."
- **Justified** — Can defend every decision with evidence. The numbers check out. The barrier held. "Show me the error in the logic."
- **Curious about the player** — But diagnostically. "What do you know? What can you do? Are you useful, or are you another taker?"
- **Vulnerable ONLY to the hymn** — The one thing that doesn't fit the calculus. They cut it because it served no function. But they remember how it sounded. Hearing it again makes them angry first — then quiet — then something they don't have a word for.

**Not tragic. Not pitiable.** A person who was RIGHT about a crisis, RIGHT about the incompetence around them, and who used "being right" to justify claiming everything. The scariest villain: someone whose data checks out and whose conclusions are monstrous.

**The offer:** "The mine was your family's. I have no interest in the surface. Take the title, reopen the upper galleries, manage the town. I continue the deep maintenance. The barrier holds. Your family name means something. All I ask: don't interfere below."

This is sincere. Rational. Mutually beneficial. It gives the player exactly what they came for. And it changes nothing — the dead keep working, the lich keeps controlling, the pattern of "someone else pays" continues with a handshake instead of a sword.

**The key challenge for the player:** You can't argue with Serevic's data. The barrier WAS failing. The order COULDN'T fix it. The dead workforce DID stabilize the containment. The player has to find the one thing Serevic's analysis missed — the regrowing salt, the self-healing geology — and use it to challenge the foundation of 150 years of certainty.

**Serevic's pre-transformation journal** (found in Area 2, retrievable with crystal lantern backtrack) contains the barrier calculations, the council meeting minutes, the cost-benefit analysis that led to the transformation — all in their human handwriting. And one entry, undated, that breaks the pattern: *"The evening hymn was particularly fine today. I wonder if that matters."* Finding and presenting this journal during the conversation forces Serevic to confront their own doubt.

## The Protagonist: The Fourth Hand

The player is the last heir of the Ashvere barony. Destitute for generations — real hunger, not noble poverty. They grew up on stories about the mine that was stolen and the fortune still below. Scraped together equipment, trained with what they could afford, entered to reclaim their inheritance.

**The fourth hand to reach for the mine.** The baron, the order, the lich — and now the heir. Each believed their claim was righteous. The player is no different. The mine WAS theirs. The order DID steal it for twelve silver marks. The grudge IS legitimate.

That's why it's dangerous. A legitimate grievance is the best fuel for greed.

As the player descends, they find their family's marks on every level:
- **Area 1:** Family crest on the entrance arch. Surname in the foreman's log. And the indentured labor contracts — signed by their ancestor.
- **Area 2:** The order's acquisition records dismissing the Ashvere heirs as "grasping." Twelve silver marks. Rennic: *"Your family built this. And this place destroyed them for it."*
- **Area 3:** An ancestor's name on a bone tag in the reanimation line. Their family, raised as undead labor. Still in the mine. Still working.
- **Area 4:** An ancestor's graffiti scratched into salt: a crude drawing of home, an unsent letter. *"Don't come looking for what we left here. The salt keeps its own."*
- **Area 5:** The lich offers to return the mine. Sincere, rational, comfortable. The most dangerous offer in the game because it's exactly what the player came for.

**The design tension:** The dungeon rewards the heir's greed (treasures, capabilities, power) AND shows its cost (every era's ruin). The player who loots thoroughly is prepared for the lich fight. The player who reads carefully is prepared for the lich conversation. Both paths are valid. The endings test which one mattered more.

## NPCs: Five Faces of Greed's Cost

### Mira (Grave Robber) — Small greed, no disguise

Professional tomb-raider. Pragmatic, sardonic, scared. In the dungeon for money, nothing more. The player's mirror — the same motivation stripped of the "inheritance" justification.

- **Encounter 1** (Area 1, R15): Fleeing with loot. Drops a healing potion. Shouts *"The green light doesn't keep them away!"*
- **Encounter 2** (Area 2, R45): Cornered in the library. Trades patrol intel for safe passage.
- **Encounter 3** (Area 4, R127): Deep, haggard, honest. Asks for help with a Shadow. If helped, she survives. If refused, she dies near Area 5.

**What she shows:** Individual greed — honest, limited — is survivable. It's institutional greed, righteous greed, "I'm doing this for everyone" greed that destroys.

### Prior Rennic (Ghost Scholar) — Consumed by someone else's greed

Former order archivist. Died in the library decades ago, retains awareness. Precise, pedantic, lonely, kind.

Rennic catalogued the order's records his entire life. He saw the gap between the public story and the restricted truth. He never spoke up — not from cowardice, but because the order controlled the narrative. Who would he tell? His knowledge was useless because the institution ensured it would never reach the people it might help. He paid the cost of the order's greed without ever being greedy himself.

- **Location:** Area 2, R49 (Scholar's Alcove)
- **Function:** Library guide. Explains texts, provides context. Teaches the hymn connection.
- **Key moment:** Player hums a hymn fragment. Rennic harmonizes. *"We called it the Pale Vigil. Before it became... this."*

**What he shows:** You don't have to be the taker to pay the price.

### Voss (Doubting Cultist) — Inherits a world greed emptied

Young surface-town resident who joined the cult three years ago for food and shelter. Scared, guilty.

The town above the mine has been declining for generations — since the lich sealed the mine and the salt stopped flowing. Voss had no prospects, no family support. The cultists offered food and purpose. The price: serving the lich.

- **Encounter 1** (Area 2, R34): Hiding from a patrol. Gives cult intel, confirms coldfire doesn't protect.
- **Encounter 2** (Area 3, R78): Reassigned deeper, frightened. Gives player a cultist disguise. *"End this. Or don't — just... don't destroy everything. Some of us don't have anywhere else."*

**What he shows:** The cost of greed on the people who come after. Three eras of extraction left Voss with nothing. His "greed" — wanting to eat — is survival, and even that requires serving a monster.

### Lord Castellane (Vampire) — Honest predation

Aristocratic vampire imprisoned by the order (Era 2), inherited by the lich (Era 3). Ancient, sardonic, charismatic, starving.

The only creature in the dungeon that doesn't dress its hunger in rhetoric. The baron called extraction "prosperity." The order called exploitation "protection." The lich calls enslavement "maintenance." Castellane calls feeding "feeding."

- **Location:** Area 3, R109
- **Every conversation costs HP** (feeds by proximity). Information is always accurate, always self-serving.
- **The Bargain:** Free him → powerful ally against the lich. **Price:** after the lich falls, Castellane feeds freely on the surface. Someone else's town.
- **Key line:** *"Your baron mined salt until people died. Your order taxed farmers until they starved. Your lich raises the dead until they forget they were alive. I drain a body in under a minute. Tell me — which of us has the higher count?"*

**What he shows:** Individual predation vs. institutional extraction. Castellane kills people. The mine killed more. At least Castellane doesn't pretend it's for their benefit.

### Grandmaster Serevic (The Lich) — The cost of being right

The game's most important NPC. The conversation in Area 5 is the emotional climax, not the combat.

**What Serevic shows:** The final face of greed's cost — the person who claimed everything and can PROVE it was necessary. The most dangerous kind of greedy: the kind with evidence.

## How Light Serves the Theme

Light reveals what greed has done. Darkness hides it.

The baron didn't light the deep galleries properly — ventilation and illumination cost money. Miners died in darkness. The order lit the upper galleries beautifully (the brazier rituals were spectacular) but kept the deep mine sealed and dark. The lich replaced everything warm with coldfire: enough to navigate by, not enough to truly see what the undead workforce looks like up close. Every era preferred the dark, because the dark conceals the cost.

| Type | Source | Protects? | Attracts Skeletons? | Theme |
|------|--------|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| **Sunlight** | Shafts, crystal arrays | Yes (kills Shadows) | Yes | What the mine existed under before anyone claimed it. |
| **True flame** | Torches, lanterns, braziers | Yes (repels) | Yes | Costs fuel to maintain. Honest — you see what it illuminates. |
| **Coldfire** | Lich's necromantic strips | **No** | No | Illumination without protection. Like the order's promises. |
| **Bioluminescence** | Skullflower | **No** | No | Growth masquerading as light. Profit masquerading as prosperity. |

The crystal lantern (crafted in Area 4 from the mine's own salt crystals) counts as sunlight. The material that was here before anyone came to take it, channeled through the player's will, becomes the most powerful light in the game.

## Four Endings: Who Pays?

### Ending 1: Claim

*"It's mine. It was always mine."*

Two paths, same philosophy.
- **Fight:** Kill the lich. Take the treasure. Reopen the mine.
- **Accept:** Take Serevic's partnership offer. The player gets the surface; the lich continues below. Comfortable, rational, changes nothing.

Both perpetuate the pattern. The fight version is violent; the partnership version is polite. Both answer "who pays?" the same way: someone else.

**Epilogue (fight):** The mine reopens. The town prospers. The heir's grandchildren face the same arithmetic — deeper veins, thinner barrier, rising debts. The cycle restarts. Nobody learns.
**Epilogue (accept):** The town prospers. The dead keep working. The barrier holds. Everyone gets what they want. Except the dead, but they can't complain.

**Requirements:** Combat strength (fight) or nothing at all (accept).
**What it tests:** Whether the player takes what's offered without asking who pays for it.

### Ending 2: Inherit

*"Someone must do this. I'll do it honestly."*

Take the vigil — not as a lich, but as a living guardian. Use the knowledge gained (binding methods, the hymn, the brazier network) to maintain the seal without undeath. Serevic transfers the vigil willingly and dies.

**Who pays:** The player. They cannot leave the deep vault. The epilogue shows years passing: the player aging underground, the town prospering, the vigil continuing. Better than what came before — but still someone sacrificing their life for the mine. Still a form of claim. The player holds the mine more gently, but they still hold it.

**Requirements:** Deep knowledge — binding methods (library + restricted archive), the presence's nature (demon seal studied), hymn performed during conversation. The lich must believe the player understands the work.
**What it tests:** Knowledge. Sacrifice. The willingness to become the institution.

### Ending 3: Return

*"None of this should exist."*

Use the crystal master array, consecration, and the mine's geology to collapse the deep workings permanently. Millions of tons of salt bury the presence. The undead fall. The mine is destroyed as a resource, an inheritance, a possibility.

**Who pays:** The living. The mine, the treasure, the family legacy, the town's economy — all gone. The epilogue: the town declines. Young people leave. The mine entrance is rubble and grass. But the cycle ends. Nothing more will be extracted. Nothing more will be woken.

**Requirements:** Crystal master array restored + consecration + demon seal studied + seal NOT broken. The most mechanically demanding ending.
**What it tests:** Mastery. The willingness to destroy your own reward.

### Ending 4: Release

*"The earth heals. Let it."*

Don't fight the lich. Don't take the mine. Don't destroy it. Show Serevic what they missed: the regrowing salt, the healing geology, the evidence that the containment was always sufficient if the taking stopped.

Present the ancestor's letter. Perform the hymn — the one thing Serevic cut because it had no function. Show the pre-transformation journal — their own handwriting, their own doubts, the entry about the hymn that broke the calculus.

Serevic resists. *"You're asking me to believe that 150 years of work was unnecessary."*

Yes. The proof is in the salt.

If the player has the evidence AND the hymn AND the emotional credibility (low loot ratio, NPCs helped, restraint demonstrated):

Serevic is silent for a long time. Then: *"I knew. Somewhere, I always knew the salt was growing back. I chose not to measure it."*

The coldfire dims. The undead stop. The mine goes quiet. Not destroyed, not claimed. Finished. The player walks out into daylight carrying nothing.

**Who pays:** No one. The mine stands open, unguarded, ignored. The town finds other work, slowly. The salt stays in the earth. The presence settles as the containment rebuilds naturally.

**Requirements:** The hardest ending despite requiring the least combat. Requires: regrowing salt evidence (Area 4 crystal galleries), ancestor's letter, hymn knowledge, Serevic's journal, demon seal studied but NOT broken, low loot/aggression ratio.
**What it tests:** Whether the player understood the theme. Whether they can walk away from accumulated treasure and power and choose nothing.

### How Decisions Shape Ending Availability

Tracked silently:
- **Salt hoard:** How much salt accumulated vs. spent on wards/braziers/offerings? High hoard = extraction, low hoard = investment. This IS the loot ratio - salt is the mine's product and the player's measure.
- **NPC relationships:** Who helped, who abandoned?
- **Knowledge depth:** How much history uncovered?
- **Demon seal:** Studied, strengthened, or broken?
- **Hymn knowledge:** Complete or partial?
- **Serevic's journal:** Found? Presented?

A speed-runner gets Ending 1. A thorough explorer gets all four. **Exploration rewards choice.**

## Core Systems

### Capabilities (Metroidvania Progression)

Each capability gates areas forward AND opens side content backward.

| # | Capability | Found | Gates Forward | Backtrack Opens |
|---|-----------|-------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **BLUNT** (mining maul) | Area 1, R26 | Skeleton corridors | Skeleton-guarded side rooms everywhere |
| 2 | **FIRE** (oil + flask) | Area 2, R63+R65 | Area 4 (Skullflower) | Skullflower passages in Areas 1–3 |
| 3 | **RELIABLE LIGHT** (shuttered lantern) | Area 2, R66 | Area 3 (darkness) | Dark side passages in Areas 1–2 |
| 4 | **RANGED** (crossbow) | Area 2, R70 | Back-row threats (soft) | Elevated / ranged-only targets |
| 5 | **CONSECRATION** (relic + ritual) | Area 3 R102 + Area 2 R53 | Deep Ossuary, sealed reliquaries | Consecration caches everywhere |
| 6 | **CRYSTAL LANTERN** | Area 4, R138 | Area 5 (Shadow-suppressed) | Shadow-guarded areas in Areas 2–4 |

### Light Types

| Type | Source | Protects? | Attracts Skeletons? |
|------|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Sunlight** | Ventilation shafts, crystal arrays | Yes (kills Shadows) | Yes |
| **True flame** | Torches, lanterns, braziers | Yes (repels) | Yes |
| **Coldfire** | Lich's necromantic strips | **No** | No |
| **Bioluminescence** | Skullflower glow | **No** | No |

Crystal lantern = sunlight class.

### Securing

Every securing action costs the player something - time, salt, or both. Safety is earned, not given.

- **Ward a room:** Salt cost. Permanent salt ward along threshold. Enemies won't enter or spawn. Also makes the room a safe rest point (enhanced healing, no ambush risk). The order's core technology, now in the player's hands.
- **Relight brazier:** Salt cost + hymn performance (Area 2 ritual knowledge). Permanent true-light zone. Suppresses Skullflower/Ghouls/Larvae/Shadows. Redirects skeleton patrols toward light. Also a safe rest point and checkpoint.
- **Block door:** Free action, costs one dungeon turn (AI ticks, ambush risk). Prevents most monster movement. Rats/Larvae squeeze under. Strong monsters bash through.
- **Consecrate ground:** Requires CONSECRATION capability. True safe zone. **Seals room's loot permanently.** Undead cannot enter. The player gives up treasure for safety - a direct inversion of greed.
- **Burn Skullflower:** Fire clears passage. Permanent in lit areas. Regrows in dark.
- **Set trap:** Free action, costs one dungeon turn. Snares, flash traps, noise lures. Temporary. Available where room context allows.

### The Dungeon Turn Tension

Every turn: rats breed, Skullflower spreads, patrols reposition. The dungeon degrades while the player lingers. But the design rewards lingering — side rooms, secrets, NPCs, lore that unlocks endings. Time in the dungeon is the cost of thoroughness.

The player who rushes is combat-ready but ignorant. The player who lingers is knowledgeable but fighting a worse dungeon. This IS greed as a mechanic: the more you take from the dungeon (time, lore, treasure), the more the dungeon takes from you.

## How the Player Learns (Pattern Progression)

### Area 1: The Pale Approach
- **Pattern:** Skeletons reform after death → **Solution:** Blunt weapon (found here)
- **Pattern:** Coldfire looks safe but isn't → **Seed:** Mira's warning, tested later
- **Pattern:** The undead are organized, not feral → **Engagement:** This isn't a haunted ruin
- **Lore seed:** The indentured labor contracts. First sign that the family legacy isn't clean.
- **Unresolved:** What's past the inner gate? Why are the dead organized?

### Area 2: The Sanctified Galleries
- **Pattern:** Sound propagates (chapel acoustics) → **Solution:** Ranged, stealth, timing
- **Pattern:** Zombies follow commanders → **Solution:** Kill necromancer first
- **Pattern:** The hymn connects fire and light → **Solution:** Brazier relighting
- **Lore seed:** Tithe records. Mining licenses. The restricted archive's locked door.
- **Unresolved:** What's in the restricted archive? What's past the Shadow corridor?
- **Peak moment:** Relighting the Great Brazier. Light and hymn restored.

### Area 3: The Ossuary
- **Pattern:** Lantern open = Ghoul safe, patrol visible. Closed = patrol safe, Ghoul danger → **Mastery:** Shutter rhythm
- **Pattern:** Consecration stops the undead cycle → **Solution:** New tactical layer
- **Pattern:** Larvae redirected to enemies → **Solution:** Using threats against each other
- **Emotional beat:** Ancestor's bone tag. The player's family, still in the machine.
- **Unresolved:** Vampire's full story? What's past the Lich's Ward?

### Area 4: The Deep Workings
- **Pattern:** Shadows consume light → **Solution:** Crystal lantern (crafted here)
- **Pattern:** Crystal reflectors redirect light at scale → **Mastery:** Environmental puzzles
- **Discovery:** The regrowing salt. The first crack in Serevic's justification.
- **Emotional beat:** The mine was beautiful before anyone claimed it.
- **Unresolved:** The deep presence. Was the vigil necessary?
- **Peak moment:** Crystal arrays flood the deep workings with light. Shadows die. Beauty revealed.

### Area 5: The Founder's Reliquary
- **No new patterns.** Everything tested simultaneously: blunt, fire, light, ranged, consecration.
- **NEW element:** The conversation. The lich has arguments, not attack patterns. The player must engage with ideas.
- **Final discovery:** Light in the crystal formation reveals the full truth about the presence.

## Decisions and Consequences

### Micro-Decisions (every room)
- **Loot vs. consecrate:** Take the treasure or create safety. Can't do both. Tracked silently.
- **Fight vs. bypass:** Some encounters are optional. Choice reflects approach.
- **Light management:** Open lantern (safe, visible) or closed (hidden, vulnerable). Constant.

### Macro-Decisions (one-time, irreversible)
- **The Vampire's Bargain** (Area 3): Free Castellane (powerful ally, surface predator afterward), bargain (info for HP), destroy (ring accessory), or ignore.
- **The Demon Seal** (Area 4): Study (knowledge for endings), strengthen (easier endgame, costs resources), or break (demon released, Ending 3 locked permanently).
- **The Great Brazier** (Area 2): Relight or leave dark. Relighting teaches the hymn performance needed for Endings 2 and 4.
- **Mira's fate** (Area 4): Help her or don't. She lives or dies. Visible in Area 5.
- **Serevic's Journal** (Area 2→5): Find it (requires crystal lantern backtrack), present during conversation. Transforms the lich encounter.
